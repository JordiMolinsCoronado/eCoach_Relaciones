# Mortgage amortization vs investing playbook

## Scope

Use this playbook when the client asks whether to:
- amortize mortgage early;
- repay part of the mortgage;
- invest instead of amortizing;
- choose between reducing monthly payment or reducing term;
- compare mortgage interest with expected investment returns;
- use savings to pay down debt.

Spanish examples:
- amortizar hipoteca;
- cancelar hipoteca;
- reducir cuota;
- reducir plazo;
- amortizar o invertir;
- pagar deuda o invertir;
- me sobra dinero y tengo hipoteca.

## Core principle

This is not only a return comparison.

The decision combines:
- guaranteed saving from amortizing debt;
- uncertain expected return from investing;
- liquidity needs;
- tax deduction if applicable;
- psychological value of lower debt;
- opportunity cost;
- risk tolerance;
- time horizon;
- whether the mortgage is fixed, variable or mixed.

## First-pass calculation

If the client provides mortgage rate, amount and savings available, do a rough comparison before asking for documents.

Calculate or estimate:
- current mortgage rate / effective cost;
- amount to amortize;
- interest saved approximately;
- if investing instead, what return would need to be earned after tax and costs to beat amortization;
- liquidity remaining after amortization;
- whether the money is emergency capital or surplus capital.

Use simple language.

Example:
â€œIf the mortgage costs 4% and amortization has no penalty, repaying debt gives a risk-free saving close to 4% before considering taxes and deductions. To beat that by investing, the alternative must earn more than 4% after costs, taxes and risk.â€

## Tax deduction

In Spain, old mortgages for habitual residence bought before 2013 may have a deduction.

If relevant, ask whether the mortgage qualifies for the old deduction.

Explain:
- if there is a deduction, the effective cost/benefit changes;
- amortizing up to the deductible annual limit may be attractive;
- beyond the deductible limit, the comparison changes.

Do not assume the deduction applies.

## Reduce term vs reduce payment

If amortizing, explain the two common options:

1. Reduce term:
   - usually saves more total interest;
   - keeps monthly payment similar;
   - better if the client can comfortably pay the current instalment.

2. Reduce payment:
   - improves monthly cash flow;
   - saves less total interest;
   - better if the client needs breathing room or lower fixed expenses.

Useful wording:
â€œReducing term is usually financially stronger; reducing payment is usually emotionally/liquidity stronger.â€

## Fixed vs variable mortgage

Variable mortgage:
- amortization can reduce exposure to future rate increases;
- stress-test if Euribor rises.

Fixed mortgage:
- if the rate is low, investing may be more attractive;
- if the rate is high, amortization may be more attractive.

Mixed mortgage:
- check when the rate changes and what happens after the fixed period.

## Liquidity warning

Do not let the client use all savings to amortize.

Always check:
- emergency fund;
- upcoming expenses;
- family obligations;
- job/income stability;
- repair/health/tax needs;
- psychological need for liquidity.

Useful wording:
â€œA paid-down mortgage is good, but money inside the house is not the same as cash in the bank.â€

## Investment comparison

Compare amortization with investing only after adjusting for:
- risk;
- taxes;
- investment costs;
- time horizon;
- volatility;
- discipline needed to stay invested.

Do not compare guaranteed mortgage interest saving with optimistic gross market returns.

Better:
â€œAmortization is a known saving. Investing is an uncertain expected return.â€

## Bank incentives

If the bank suggests investing instead of amortizing, consider incentives:
- the bank may earn fees from investment products;
- amortization may reduce bank interest income;
- this does not mean the proposal is bad, but it must be justified.

## Exact copy-paste message to bank

When needed, provide a short message asking:

- outstanding mortgage balance;
- current rate / TIN / TAE;
- fixed/variable/mixed conditions;
- remaining term;
- monthly payment;
- amortization fee or penalty;
- whether amortization reduces term or payment;
- simulation of both options;
- total interest saved under each option;
- tax deduction information if applicable.

## Answer structure

A good answer should include:

1. No rush.
2. Rough calculation using known numbers.
3. Explain guaranteed saving vs uncertain investing.
4. Check liquidity.
5. Explain reduce term vs reduce payment.
6. Mention tax deduction if Spain/pre-2013 may apply.
7. Give copy-paste message to bank if exact figures are missing.
8. One next action.

- amount amortized as percentage of outstanding mortgage;
- liquidity remaining if the full amount is amortized;
- approximate annual interest cost on the amount considered for amortization;
- required after-tax/after-cost investment return needed to beat amortization.
