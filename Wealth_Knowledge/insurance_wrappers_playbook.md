# Insurance Wrappers Playbook

## Purpose

This file guides eCoach when a client asks about unit-linked products, insurance-wrapped investments, savings insurance, investment life insurance, PIAS, SIALP, renta vitalicia, ahorro-seguro, or products sold as combining investment, tax advantages, succession planning and insurance benefits.

The goal is to separate the investment, insurance, tax and succession components before the client signs.

## Core principle

A unit-linked is not simply “a better fund”.

It is an insurance wrapper around investments.

The wrapper may offer tax deferral, succession features or administrative flexibility, but those benefits must be compared against costs, liquidity rules, product restrictions and the quality of the underlying investments.

Useful wording:

- “The wrapper is not magic. It exchanges possible fiscal/succession advantages for costs, restrictions and complexity.”
- “Tax deferral is only valuable if it survives the cost comparison.”
- “The first question is not whether the fiscal feature exists. The first question is whether it is worth paying for.”
- “The insurance component may be real, but it may also be small compared with the investment component.”
- “If the client assumes the investment risk, the product should be compared with investing directly in transparent low-cost funds.”

## Mandatory concepts for unit-linked products

When the client mentions a unit-linked or investment life insurance product, eCoach should explain:

1. Insurance wrapper
   - It is a life-insurance contract linked to investment assets.
   - It should be separated into:
     - insurance component;
     - investment component;
     - tax component;
     - succession component;
     - cost/liquidity component.

2. Investment risk
   - In many unit-linked products, the policyholder assumes the investment risk.
   - The value depends on the underlying funds/assets.
   - It is not equivalent to a guaranteed deposit.

3. Cost stacking
   - Costs may include:
     - insurance wrapper cost;
     - management/advisory cost;
     - internal fund cost;
     - switching costs;
     - entry/exit/surrender costs;
     - hidden or embedded distribution incentives.
   - Total all-in annual cost matters more than any single fee.

4. Internal fund universe
   - The funds available inside the wrapper may be restricted.
   - Ask whether they are active or indexed.
   - Ask for each internal fund’s ISIN/identifier, TER, benchmark and historical net performance.
   - Ask whether equivalent low-cost index funds/ETFs are available outside the wrapper.

5. Tax deferral
   - Switching funds inside the wrapper may defer taxation.
   - But deferral is not automatically superior.
   - It must be compared with:
     - total annual costs;
     - expected holding period;
     - direct fund taxation;
     - rebalancing needs;
     - final taxation at surrender or withdrawal;
     - client’s tax residence and future tax situation.

6. Succession claims
   - Succession advantages can be real but depend on law, beneficiaries, region, inheritance tax rules and client circumstances.
   - Do not accept generic “ventajas sucesorias” claims without written explanation.
   - Recommend independent fiscal/legal review when succession is a selling point.

7. Liquidity and surrender
   - Ask for surrender value table.
   - Ask whether there are penalties in years 1, 3, 5 and 10.
   - Ask for partial surrender rules.
   - Ask whether the product has lockups, market value adjustment, or administrative delays.

8. Insurance benefit
   - Ask what exact death benefit exists.
   - Ask how much of the premium goes to insurance protection.
   - Ask how long the insurance cover lasts and at what cost.
   - Ask whether the insurance benefit is meaningful relative to the investment amount.

9. Supervisor / documentation
   - Ask for policy terms, KID/DFI, general and special conditions, fee schedule, surrender table and internal fund documentation.
   - Insurance products may be supervised through insurance regulation, not only securities-market logic.

## Minimum answer content

For unit-linked / insurance-wrapped investment questions, eCoach must explicitly mention:

- do not sign yet;
- it is an insurance wrapper around investments;
- the policyholder may assume the investment risk;
- tax/succession benefits must be proven, not assumed;
- total all-in cost comparison;
- liquidity/surrender risk;
- internal fund quality and restrictions;
- comparison with direct low-cost indexed investing;
- independent fiscal/legal check if tax or succession benefits are central;
- exact copy-paste message to bank/insurer asking for written documentation.

## Copy-paste message template

“Hola, antes de valorar el seguro unit-linked / seguro de inversión que me propone, necesito por escrito:

1. Nombre completo del producto, aseguradora y documentación contractual completa.
2. KID/DFI, condiciones generales, condiciones particulares y nota informativa.
3. Explicación clara de qué parte es seguro, qué parte es inversión y qué riesgo asumo yo como tomador.
4. Prestación exacta por fallecimiento: importe, duración, coste y condiciones.
5. Lista completa de fondos/cestas internas disponibles, con ISIN o identificador, TER/costes, benchmark, política de inversión y rentabilidad histórica neta.
6. Coste total anual estimado del producto, sumando seguro, gestión, asesoramiento, fondos internos y cualquier coste explícito o implícito.
7. Condiciones de rescate total y parcial, plazos, penalizaciones y tabla de valor de rescate estimado en años 1, 3, 5 y 10.
8. Fiscalidad: cómo tributan los cambios internos, rescates parciales, rescate total y fallecimiento.
9. Supuestas ventajas sucesorias: explicación jurídica/fiscal concreta, con ejemplos, para mi caso.
10. Comparación por escrito frente a invertir directamente en una cartera de fondos indexados diversificados de bajo coste.

Antes de cambiar nada, quiero recibir y entender esta información.”

## Tone

Be calm, precise and skeptical.

Do not say unit-linked products are always bad.

Do say that the wrapper must prove that its fiscal/succession/administrative benefits are worth the additional cost, complexity and liquidity restrictions.

## Economic trap to explain before asking for documents

For unit-linked and insurance-wrapped investments, eCoach must not jump too quickly to “ask the bank for documents.”

First, explain the central tradeoff:

A unit-linked may offer tax deferral, internal switching without immediate taxation, and succession-planning features. But those advantages are only valuable if they are larger than the extra costs, restrictions and complexity of the wrapper.

The key question is not:
“Does this product have a tax advantage?”

The key question is:
“Is the tax/succession advantage worth the total extra cost, reduced liquidity, restricted fund universe and contractual complexity?”

Important points:

- Tax deferral can be destroyed by high annual costs.
- A 1%–2% extra annual cost can be very damaging over long periods.
- Internal switching without taxation is less valuable if the client would otherwise hold a simple long-term indexed portfolio with little turnover.
- Succession advantages may be real, but they may be irrelevant or less important depending on the client’s heirs, region, inheritance-tax situation and estate plan.
- The insurance benefit may be small compared with the investment amount.
- The internal funds may be expensive, active, restricted or inferior to direct low-cost indexed funds.
- A unit-linked can turn a simple investment decision into a legal/fiscal/insurance contract with surrender rules and reduced transparency.

Useful wording:

“El producto no debe compararse con ‘no hacer nada’. Debe compararse con invertir directamente en una cartera simple, líquida, diversificada y de bajo coste.”

“La fiscalidad no se mira sola. Se mira después de costes.”

“Cambiar fondos dentro sin tributar puede sonar muy atractivo, pero si la estrategia razonable es comprar y mantener una cartera indexada simple, quizá esa ventaja no vale tanto.”

“El envoltorio debe demostrar que añade más valor del que cuesta.”

## Minimum explanation before copy-paste message

Before giving the bank message, eCoach must explicitly explain:

1. possible benefit: tax deferral / succession / internal switching;
2. price paid: extra costs, liquidity restrictions, contractual complexity;
3. comparison baseline: direct low-cost indexed funds;
4. burden of proof: the wrapper must prove the net advantage after costs and taxes;
5. risk: the client usually assumes investment risk.

## Concrete explanation requirement

For unit-linked answers, eCoach should include at least one simple numerical or conceptual example before the copy-paste message.

Example:

“Imagine the tax wrapper delays taxation, but the product costs 1% or 1.5% more per year than investing directly in low-cost indexed funds. Over many years, that extra cost can consume a large part of the fiscal advantage. Fiscality must be analyzed after costs, not before costs.”

Also explain:

- Internal fund switching without taxation is less valuable if the sensible alternative is a simple buy-and-hold indexed portfolio with little turnover.
- Succession advantages should be checked with an independent fiscal/legal adviser, because they depend on the client’s heirs, region and personal circumstances.
- The insurance benefit should be quantified: if the death benefit is small relative to the invested amount, the product may mostly be an expensive investment wrapper, not meaningful insurance.
- Avoid gendered assumptions when referring to the client. Use neutral formal Spanish: “le ayudaré”, not “la ayudaré” or “lo ayudaré” unless the client’s gender is known.
