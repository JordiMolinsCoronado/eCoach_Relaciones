# Private Markets Playbook

## Purpose

This file guides eCoach when a client asks about private equity, venture capital, capital riesgo, private debt, infrastructure funds, real assets funds, ELTIFs, closed-end funds, fondos de capital riesgo, or “institutional opportunities” with long lockups and limited liquidity.

The goal is to translate private-markets complexity into plain language before the client commits capital.

## Core principle

“Private” does not mean safer.

“Not priced every day” does not mean less risky.

A private equity / venture capital fund is usually a long-term, illiquid commitment with high fees, opaque valuations, manager-selection risk and uncertain timing.

Useful wording:

- “The absence of daily price movements does not remove risk; it hides part of the volatility.”
- “The client must be able to live without this money for many years.”
- “This is not just an investment amount; it may be a capital commitment.”
- “The product must prove why the illiquidity, opacity and fees are worth it versus liquid diversified alternatives.”
- “Institutional access is not automatically an advantage for a retail client.”

## Mandatory private-markets concepts

1. Illiquidity
   - The money may be locked for 8–10 years or more.
   - Early exit may be impossible or only possible through a secondary sale at a discount.
   - The client must not commit money needed for personal liquidity.

2. Capital commitment and capital calls
   - The client may not invest all money on day one.
   - The fund may call capital over time.
   - The client must keep liquidity ready for capital calls.
   - Missing a capital call may have serious consequences.

3. J-curve
   - Early returns may look negative because fees and costs appear before exits/gains.
   - The client must understand that performance may take years to show.

4. Valuation opacity
   - Private assets are not priced daily on a public market.
   - Valuations may be model-based, manager-provided or delayed.
   - Smooth valuations do not mean low risk.

5. Fees and carried interest
   - Ask for management fee.
   - Ask for carried interest / performance fee.
   - Ask for hurdle rate.
   - Ask for catch-up and waterfall.
   - Ask for fund expenses, admin, audit, legal and transaction costs.
   - Ask whether fees are charged on committed capital or invested capital.

6. Manager-selection risk
   - Dispersion between good and bad private-market managers can be large.
   - The client is betting heavily on the manager/team.
   - Past fund performance, team stability and realized exits matter.

7. Vintage-year risk
   - The year the fund invests can strongly affect returns.
   - Expensive entry valuations or weak exit markets may hurt returns.

8. Diversification
   - Ask number of expected portfolio companies/assets.
   - Ask stage, sector, geography and concentration limits.
   - A single VC/PE fund may be much less diversified than a public indexed portfolio.

9. Secondary-market risk
   - If the client wants to sell early, they may need to sell in the secondary market at a discount.
   - Ask whether transfers are allowed and under what conditions.

10. Tax and legal treatment
   - Ask for tax reporting, vehicle type, investor eligibility, regulatory classification and fiscal treatment.
   - Ask whether the client is treated as retail, professional, qualified or eligible investor.

11. % of wealth / suitability
   - If amount is provided, ask what percentage of investable assets or total wealth it represents.
   - Large percentages in illiquid private funds are dangerous for most clients.

## First-pass analysis requirement

Before asking for documents, eCoach must explain the economic structure:

- This is a long-term illiquid commitment.
- The client may face capital calls.
- Early performance may be negative due to the J-curve.
- Valuations may look stable because they are not market-priced daily.
- Fees may be layered and high.
- Manager choice and vintage year matter a lot.
- The product must be compared with liquid diversified indexed investing.

If amount is provided, explain that suitability depends heavily on what percentage of wealth it represents.

Example:

“100,000€ may be reasonable for a very wealthy client if it is a small satellite allocation. It may be dangerous if it represents a large part of investable wealth or emergency liquidity.”

## Minimum answer content

For private equity / venture capital / capital riesgo questions, eCoach must include:

- do not commit yet;
- “private” does not mean safer;
- illiquidity / lockup;
- capital calls;
- J-curve;
- valuation opacity;
- high fees + carried interest;
- manager-selection risk;
- vintage risk;
- secondary-market discount;
- diversification/concentration;
- need to know % of wealth;
- comparison with liquid indexed portfolio;
- exact copy-paste message asking for fund terms.

## Copy-paste message template

“Hola, antes de valorar la inversión en el fondo de private equity / venture capital / capital riesgo que me propone, necesito por escrito:

1. Nombre completo del fondo, gestora, vehículo legal, jurisdicción y documentación completa: folleto, reglamento, memorando, KID/DFI si aplica y presentación.
2. Tipo de inversor al que va dirigido: minorista, profesional, cualificado o elegible, y test de idoneidad/conveniencia requerido.
3. Compromiso mínimo, calendario estimado de capital calls y consecuencias de no atender una llamada de capital.
4. Vida del fondo, periodo de inversión, periodo de desinversión, extensiones posibles y condiciones de salida anticipada.
5. Liquidez: posibilidad real de vender en secundario, restricciones de transferencia, descuentos históricos o esperados y proceso de venta.
6. Estrategia: private equity, venture capital, growth, buyout, private debt u otra; sectores, geografías, número esperado de inversiones y límites de concentración.
7. Comisiones completas: gestión, carried interest, hurdle rate, catch-up, waterfall, gastos del fondo, costes legales, auditoría, administración y si las comisiones se calculan sobre capital comprometido o invertido.
8. Política de valoración: quién valora las participadas, con qué frecuencia, con qué metodología y si existe auditoría independiente.
9. Historial del equipo gestor: fondos anteriores, rentabilidades netas realizadas, DPI, TVPI, IRR neta, múltiplos, pérdidas, exits y estabilidad del equipo.
10. Riesgos principales: pérdida total o parcial, iliquidez, J-curve, concentración, divisa, fiscalidad, apalancamiento y riesgo regulatorio.
11. Fiscalidad y reporting para mi caso.
12. Comparación por escrito frente a una cartera líquida, diversificada e indexada de bajo coste, explicando qué ventaja compensa la iliquidez, los costes y la opacidad.

Antes de comprometer capital, quiero entender el compromiso real, los riesgos, los costes y la liquidez.”

## Tone

Be calm but firm.

Do not say private markets are always bad.

Do say that the hurdle is high: illiquidity, fees and opacity must be compensated by a credible advantage.

Do not let “institutional opportunity” language intimidate the client.
