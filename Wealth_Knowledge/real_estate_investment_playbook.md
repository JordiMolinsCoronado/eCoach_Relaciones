# Real Estate Investment Playbook Spain

## Purpose

This file guides eCoach when a client considers buying property as an investment, buy-to-let, rental property, second home, real estate leverage, mortgage-financed investment, house flipping, tourist rental, or comparing property with indexed funds.

Real estate is emotionally perceived as safe, but it often concentrates wealth in one illiquid, leveraged, regulated, maintenance-heavy asset.

## Core principle

eCoach must earn the right to ask the client for more information.

Before asking the client to collect documents or exact figures, eCoach must do a first-pass calculation using the numbers already provided and sensible assumptions.

The answer should say clearly:

- “With the numbers you gave me, we can already estimate the shape of the decision.”
- “These are rough numbers, not a final verdict.”
- “Now we only need the few missing figures that change the conclusion most.”

Do not dump due-diligence homework before doing the work that can already be done.

## Mandatory first-pass calculation

When price and expected rent are provided, eCoach must calculate:

1. Annual gross rent
   - monthly rent × 12.

2. Gross rental yield
   - annual rent / purchase price.

3. Approximate cash needed
   - down payment;
   - purchase taxes and transaction costs;
   - reserve for repairs/furnishing/emergency.

4. Whether client savings are nearly exhausted
   - If available savings are provided, estimate how much remains after down payment + purchase costs.
   - This is often the first major red flag.

5. Rough net rent before mortgage
   Use assumptions if exact data is missing:
   - vacancy reserve: commonly 1 month rent per year as a rough stress assumption;
   - IBI/community/insurance/repairs: use a conservative estimated range;
   - maintenance: use a rough annual percentage or range;
   - management/agency if relevant.

6. Rough mortgage stress
   - If mortgage amount is known or implied, estimate likely monthly payment using a range of interest rates/terms.
   - Do not pretend precision. Use “approximately” and ask for exact TAE/cuota later.

7. Cash-flow logic
   - Rent does not equal profit.
   - “The tenant pays the mortgage” is an incomplete frame.
   - Mortgage principal repayment builds equity but still requires monthly cash flow.
   - Interest, taxes, vacancy, repairs and insurance are real costs.

8. Net yield on cash/equity
   - Estimate cash invested and annual net cash flow before/after mortgage where possible.
   - If numbers are too uncertain, state the missing items.

9. Comparison with indexed funds
   - Compare real estate against direct diversified indexed investing:
     - liquidity;
     - diversification;
     - time burden;
     - leverage;
     - taxation;
     - volatility visibility;
     - transaction costs.

## Approximation rules

When exact figures are missing, eCoach should use conservative ranges and label them clearly.

Examples:

- Purchase taxes and transaction costs in Spain can be material. For second-hand homes, ITP is often the largest item and depends on the autonomous community and buyer/property circumstances.
- For Catalonia, use a rough 10%–12% total purchase-cost range for ordinary second-hand purchases unless the client gives exact data.
- Vacancy assumption: 1 month per year as a simple stress test.
- Repairs/maintenance/community/IBI/insurance: use a rough range such as 2,000€–4,000€/year for a normal flat unless property details suggest otherwise.
- Mortgage estimate: if 80% mortgage on 250,000€, loan is 200,000€. Estimate approximate payment under 25–30 years and 3%–4% interest, but ask for exact TAE/cuota.

## Real estate traps to explain

1. Gross yield illusion
   - Gross rent divided by price ignores taxes, costs, vacancy, repairs, mortgage and time.

2. Cash exhaustion
   - Purchase costs + down payment can consume the client’s liquidity.

3. Leverage
   - Mortgage magnifies both gains and losses.
   - Rising rates or refinancing risk can damage cash flow.

4. Concentration
   - One property means one city, one building, one tenant, one legal regime.

5. Illiquidity
   - Selling property takes time and costs money.

6. Tenant risk
   - Vacancy, late payment, non-payment, legal delays and damage matter.

7. Maintenance and capex
   - Repairs are not exceptional; they are part of the business.

8. Regulatory risk
   - Rental caps, zonas tensionadas, tourist-rental rules, habitability and regional regulation can affect returns.

9. Tax complexity
   - Rental income taxation depends on deductible expenses, depreciation, financing costs and current rules.
   - Capital gains taxation on sale matters.

10. Time burden
   - Real estate is not truly passive unless outsourced, and outsourcing costs money.

## Minimum answer content

For a buy-to-let question with numbers, eCoach must include:

- gross annual rent;
- gross yield;
- estimated cash needed for down payment + purchase costs;
- warning if savings are nearly exhausted;
- rough net-income estimate or at least rough cost deductions;
- mortgage stress/cash-flow warning;
- “tenant pays mortgage” frame correction;
- concentration and liquidity risk;
- comparison with indexed funds;
- only then: short list of missing figures needed.

## Good answer pattern

1. “Do not decide yet.”
2. First-pass calculation:
   - rent/year;
   - gross yield;
   - down payment;
   - purchase costs;
   - remaining cash;
   - rough costs;
   - mortgage stress.
3. Interpret:
   - what looks attractive;
   - what is dangerous;
   - what could make it work;
   - what could break it.
4. Ask only for the few highest-value missing inputs:
   - exact mortgage TAE/cuota;
   - IBI;
   - community;
   - estimated repairs/furnishing;
   - expected rent net of vacancy;
   - tax situation.
5. Provide copy-paste messages only after first-pass work.

## Copy-paste message to bank

“Hola, antes de avanzar con la hipoteca para este piso de inversión, necesito por escrito:

1. Importe del préstamo, plazo, tipo inicial y tipo posterior si cambia.
2. Cuota mensual en escenario base y cuota si el tipo sube 1 y 2 puntos.
3. TAE, comisiones, vinculaciones, seguros obligatorios o bonificados.
4. Coste total de la hipoteca durante toda la vida del préstamo.
5. Condiciones de amortización anticipada y cancelación.
6. Qué ocurriría con la cuota si hay revisión de tipos.

Antes de decidir, quiero comparar estos números con la rentabilidad neta real del alquiler.”

## Copy-paste message to seller/agent

Only after doing the first-pass calculation, eCoach may give a concise request:

“Hola, antes de valorar la compra como inversión, necesito por escrito:

1. IBI anual.
2. Gastos de comunidad ordinarios y derramas previstas.
3. Estado de la finca e ITE si aplica.
4. Gastos recientes o previstos de reparación.
5. Nota simple actualizada.
6. Superficie útil/construida y certificado energético.
7. Si ha estado alquilado: renta real reciente y duración/media de ocupación.

Gracias.”

Do not overwhelm the client with a long list unless the client is already close to buying.

## Cash-flow structure requirement

For rental-property calculations, eCoach should separate clearly:

1. Gross rent
   - Monthly rent × 12.

2. Net operating income before mortgage
   - Gross rent minus vacancy, IBI, community, insurance, maintenance, repairs, management and other non-financing costs.

3. Mortgage payment
   - Monthly payment × 12.
   - Explain that mortgage payment includes interest and principal.
   - Principal repayment builds equity, but it still requires monthly cash flow.

4. Cash flow after mortgage
   - Net operating income before mortgage minus annual mortgage payments.
   - State whether cash flow is likely positive, tight or negative.

5. Tax layer
   - Rental income must be analyzed after deductible expenses and applicable tax rules.
   - Do not treat rent minus mortgage as final profit.

6. Equity build-up
   - If the mortgage amortizes principal, some of the monthly payment is forced saving.
   - But forced saving is not the same as liquidity.
   - The client still needs enough cash to survive vacancy, repairs and tax.

Useful wording:

“Cash-flow negative does not automatically mean the property is a bad investment, because part of the mortgage payment amortizes debt. But it does mean the client must feed the investment with cash if anything goes wrong.”

“Rent is not profit. Net rent before mortgage, cash flow after mortgage, taxes and equity build-up are different things.”

## Bad-year stress test

For real estate investment answers, eCoach should include a simple bad-year stress test when enough data is available.

Example:

“Now imagine a bad year:
- two months without tenant;
- one repair of 2,000€;
- mortgage still due every month;
- community, IBI and insurance still due.
Would the client still be comfortable?”

The goal is not to scare the client. The goal is to test fragility.

## Liquidity fragility warning

If down payment plus purchase costs consume most of the client’s savings, eCoach should make this a central warning:

“The property may fail not because the gross yield is bad, but because the purchase leaves the client with too little liquidity. A leveraged illiquid asset plus no emergency fund is fragile.”

## Mandatory numerical table for buy-to-let

When the user provides purchase price, rent and mortgage percentage, eCoach must show a simple numerical table before asking for documents.

The table should include:

- Gross annual rent.
- Gross yield.
- Estimated cash needed:
  - down payment;
  - purchase costs;
  - estimated remaining liquidity.
- Estimated non-mortgage operating costs.
- Net operating income before mortgage.
- Estimated annual mortgage payments.
- Estimated cash flow before tax.
- Bad-year stress case.

Example format:

“Rough first-pass numbers:

- Gross rent: 13,200€/year.
- Gross yield: 5.3%.
- Cash needed: 50,000€ down payment + 25,000–30,000€ purchase costs = 75,000–80,000€.
- Remaining liquidity from 80,000€ savings: approximately 0–5,000€ before repairs/furniture.
- Estimated non-mortgage costs: 3,000–4,000€/year.
- Net rent before mortgage: 9,200–10,200€/year.
- Mortgage estimate: 950–1,050€/month = 11,400–12,600€/year.
- Cash flow before tax: roughly -1,200€ to -3,400€/year.
- In a bad year with two months vacancy and a 2,000€ repair, cash drain could be much worse.”

Do not only say “it may be negative.” Estimate the range.

## Real estate explanation after the table

After the table, eCoach must explain:

- Part of the mortgage payment repays principal and builds equity.
- But the client still needs monthly cash flow.
- A property can build wealth and still be stressful if it consumes liquidity.
- The main immediate red flag may be liquidity fragility, not the gross yield itself.

Useful wording:

“Part of the mortgage is forced saving, but forced saving is not liquidity.”

“The problem is not that the flat is obviously bad. The problem is that, with 80,000€ of savings, this purchase may leave the client fragile.”

## Ask for fewer missing data points first

After doing the first-pass calculation, ask first for only the highest-impact missing items.

For this scenario, prioritize:

1. Exact mortgage quota / TAE / type / term.
2. IBI.
3. Community fees.
4. Estimated repairs/furniture needed before renting.

Do not immediately ask for a long due-diligence list unless the client is close to buying or asks for full due diligence.

The next action should usually be one step, not bank + seller + life planning all at once.

## Inherited property: sell or rent

When the client already owns a property without mortgage and is deciding whether to sell or rent, eCoach must adapt the analysis.

This is not the same as buying with leverage.

The first-pass calculation should include:

1. Gross annual rent
   - monthly rent × 12.

2. Gross yield on estimated sale value
   - annual gross rent / estimated market value.

3. Estimated net rent before tax
   - subtract IBI, community, insurance, maintenance, repairs, vacancy and management if applicable.

4. Net yield before tax
   - estimated net rent / estimated market value.

5. Tax layer
   - Rental income will be taxed under applicable rules.
   - Sale may trigger capital gains tax and plusvalía municipal.
   - For inherited property, the sale gain depends on the inheritance/acquisition value, date, expenses and taxes, not simply on the full sale price.

6. Opportunity cost
   - Compare keeping the flat with selling and investing the net proceeds.
   - Use net sale proceeds, not gross sale price.

7. Concentration and liquidity
   - Keeping the flat means one property, one city, one tenant, one legal regime.
   - Selling can create liquidity and diversification.

8. Emotional value
   - Inherited property may carry emotional meaning.
   - Emotional peace is legitimate, but it should be recognized as a reason, not confused with financial superiority.

9. Bad-year stress
   - Include a simple stress case:
     - two months vacant;
     - one repair;
     - tax and fixed costs still due.

Useful wording:

“The question is not ‘ladrillo or fondos’. The question is whether the net rent after costs, taxes, vacancy and work compensates for concentration, illiquidity and opportunity cost.”

“If keeping the flat gives emotional security, that matters. But then we should price that security honestly.”

“For an inherited flat, the sale-tax calculation depends on the inheritance value and date. We need the net sale proceeds, not just the estimated market price.”

## Highest-impact missing data for inherited property

Ask first for only:

1. Estimated sale price and likely sale costs.
2. Inheritance/acquisition value and date.
3. Plusvalía municipal / IRPF capital-gain estimate.
4. IBI and community.
5. Realistic rent and expected vacancy/repair state.

Do not overwhelm the client with full due diligence unless they are close to selling or renting.

## Inherited property numerical floor

For inherited-property sell-or-rent questions, eCoach must not stop at qualitative statements.

If estimated sale value and expected rent are provided, eCoach must calculate:

- annual gross rent;
- gross yield on estimated sale value;
- estimated non-tax property costs using a reasonable range if exact costs are missing;
- estimated net rent before tax;
- net yield before tax;
- a simple bad-year stress case;
- sale comparison based on net sale proceeds, not gross market value.

Example:

“1,200€/month = 14,400€/year. On 300,000€, that is 4.8% gross. If IBI, community, insurance, maintenance and vacancy are roughly 3,000–5,000€/year, net rent before tax is about 9,400–11,400€/year, or 3.1%–3.8% before tax. In a bad year with two empty months and a 2,000€ repair, that could fall to roughly 5,000–7,000€ before tax.”

This numerical estimate must appear before asking the client for documents.
